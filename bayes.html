The Setup

We want to compute \( P(A \mid B) \), but we only know \( P(B \mid A) \). That‚Äôs where Bayes jumps in and says:

$$ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)} $$

Worked Example

Suppose 1% of people have a rare disease. A test for the disease is 99% accurate: it returns a positive result 99% of the time when someone has the disease, and 1% of the time when someone doesn't. You take the test and it's positive. What's the probability you actually have the disease?

Click to show the solution

Let:

  • \( D \): you have the disease
  • \( + \): test is positive

We‚Äôre looking for \( P(D \mid +) \). Bayes says:

$$ P(D \mid +) = \frac{P(+ \mid D) \cdot P(D)}{P(+)} $$

Now plug in the values:

  • \( P(D) = 0.01 \)
  • \( P(+ \mid D) = 0.99 \)
  • \( P(+ \mid \text{no } D) = 0.01 \)
  • \( P(\text{no } D) = 0.99 \)

$$ P(+) = P(+ \mid D) \cdot P(D) + P(+ \mid \text{no } D) \cdot P(\text{no } D) $$ $$ = 0.99 \cdot 0.01 + 0.01 \cdot 0.99 = 0.0198 $$

So: $$ P(D \mid +) = \frac{0.99 \cdot 0.01}{0.0198} = 0.5 $$

You only have a 50% chance of actually having the disease, despite the test being "99% accurate." Welcome to base rate fallacy hell üî•.
